6.S194

Syllabus

Goal

Software development outside of a university class involves code that spans decades (Windows is 27 years old!), teams that span countries, and a lot of bottom-up project management and decision-making. Our goal is to help you grow your software development skills by providing an experience that matches real world software development as closely as possible. Think of it as a bit of an internship during the semester, with a strong mentoring component. We hope that this will be a different kind of class, in a good way.

Format

This class is being taught in conjunction with several universities and open source projects. Each of you will be on a team with other students (from MIT and elsewhere) working on one of those open source projects. Your mentors will be me and a representative from the open source project. You can find the full list of projects and descriptions on the Trip / Project Sign-up Form (which has been emailed to you if you signed up).

For the first few weeks, "normal" classes will be held Monday and Wednesday. These classes will take one of two formats:

  • Hands-on lectures (blend of lecture and class exercise), which will cover:
    • Project workflow with git
    • Prototyping / software architecture skills
    • Debugging Skills
  • Guest lectures, which we expect to cover:
    • Open source business models
    • Working with remote teams

After that, class will be used as a time for us to hold mentoring meetings, and occasionally as a time for you to reflect and present your work to the class.

Grading and Effort

The majority (70%) of your grade will be based on your "commits," that is, the actual code contributions you make to the open source project you are working on and the overall project management process that you demonstrate. Our goal is to provide you a real-world learning experience, and so we are trying to grade you the way managers would.

The rest of your grade (30%) will be based on class participation, attendance, group performance, and a few smaller assignments that we will do.

Scoping the work (what to do, how much to do) is something each of you will be in charge of and we will help you with. We want you to work hard and be proud of shipping code that will actually get used. Each open source mentor has already compiled an introduction to the project and description of some project ideas for you, available on the Trip/Project Sign-up form (emailed to you).

Credit

This class provides 12 credits and 12 Engineering Design Points. It counts as a lab for EECS.
